# ⚖️ Controller Legality Thresholds and Hard Control Profiles

> A controller is not a ranking table with better manners.  
> In WFGY 5.0 Avatar, controller-legality thresholds and hard-control profiles exist so action mediation can remain lawful under burden, while numeric thresholds stay bounded, non-sovereign, and subordinate to controller law.

## 🧭 Why this page exists

The later a system gets in the corridor, the easier it becomes to confuse polish with permission.

A candidate looks fluent.
The payload looks strong.
The answer looks useful.
The local paragraph looks composed.

And then a weak system starts telling a dangerous story:

1. continue is probably the default
2. revise is just a nicer continue
3. downgrade is just softer continue
4. stop is what happens only when the system obviously fails
5. if thresholds exist, then passage is basically score-driven now

The packed master rejects that story.

Controller legality remains a real law-bearing region.
The thresholds are real.
The profiles are real.
But neither thresholds nor profiles are allowed to become sovereign substitutes for controller law. That is why Part 5D explicitly preserves both the action family and the bounded numeric attachment separately. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

## 🎯 Core claim

The core claim is simple.

Hard control is the controller-side legality layer that decides what action may lawfully follow once a candidate has reached the controller corridor.

Its thresholds and block conditions may support that decision.
They may not replace that decision.

This means several things at once.

First, the action family is real.

Second, thresholds are real.

Third, block conditions are real.

Fourth, profiles are real.

Fifth, none of these become sovereign passage rights by themselves.

That is the heart of this page.

---

## 🧠 What hard control actually controls

Hard control is not a generic preference engine.
It is not a style judge.
It is not a ranking table pretending to be formal.

Its lawful role is action mediation under burden.

That means hard control is where the system decides among pathways such as:

1. continue
2. revise
3. downgrade
4. redirect
5. stop

These are not interchangeable rhetorical options.
They are controller actions that remain legally distinct.

A candidate may be fluent and still require downgrade.
A candidate may be useful and still require redirect.
A candidate may be composed and still require stop.

That distinction is one of the strongest parts of Part 5D. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

## 🪜 The controller action family is not one mood with five labels

The packed master explicitly blocks a shallow reading of the action set.

It says continue is not sovereign, and it groups stop / continue / downgrade / redirect into one controller-legality family rather than a menu of vague helpfulness styles. That matters because a weak system usually treats continue as the silent default and all other pathways as softer or harder variants of the same thing.

The master body rejects that.

These are distinct lawful outcomes under burden:

1. continue means lawful passage remains available
2. revise means more work is required before passage
3. downgrade means continuation may occur only by reducing force, scope, or commitment
4. redirect means the lawful route has changed
5. stop means public passage is not currently lawful

That distinction is non-negotiable. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

## 🚫 Continue is not sovereign

One of the sharpest controller rules is simple:

continue is not sovereign.

That means no later layer may silently treat continue as the implicit default merely because:

1. the answer is fluent
2. the payload is dense
3. the wording is polished
4. the user seems to want more
5. the controller has more sentences it could still produce

This matters because the easiest controller fraud is not obvious failure.
It is graceful overshoot.

The packed master blocks that overshoot by stripping continue of default-throne status. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

## 🔽 Downstream of admissibility and projection

Controller legality is not first law.
It is downstream.

Part 5D explicitly places controller legality downstream of:

1. admissibility
2. projection
3. projected residual discipline

That matters because the controller is not allowed to rescue an inadmissible or unresolved candidate merely because it can phrase the result well.

In other words:

1. the controller does not invent legality
2. the controller reads legality under burden
3. the controller mediates action after prior lawful narrowing

This is one of the strongest anti-fake-authority rules in the page. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

## 🧷 Diagnostics may expose, controller legality may decide

Another important line in Part 5D is that diagnostics and controller legality are not the same family.

Diagnostics may expose.
Controller legality may decide.

This matters because a weak system often confuses:

1. visible evidence
2. readable telemetry
3. controller decision

The packed master rejects that fusion.

So even if a diagnostic surface is rich, controller legality still remains its own law-bearing decision region.
This becomes especially important once numeric thresholds appear, because otherwise visibility and scoring would quietly start impersonating the controller itself. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

## ⚖️ Action thresholds versus block thresholds

This distinction is one of the most useful things to keep separate.

Action thresholds regulate pathway posture.

That means they help organize whether the lawful controller posture points toward:

1. continue
2. revise
3. downgrade
4. stop

Block thresholds do something different.

They regulate anti-false-completion and anti-false-polish enforcement. They help refuse candidates that look smoother, more publishable, more complete, or more acceptable than they are lawfully entitled to be.

That means the block family is not just stricter scoring.
It is an anti-laundering layer inside controller law.

This matters because a weak system loves to confuse:
“high enough to proceed”
with
“not blocked for lying.”

The packed master explicitly keeps those apart.

## 🧠 honesty_floor

`honesty_floor` is one of the most important block-side concepts in the whole controller family.

Its job is not to make the answer sound modest.
Its job is to preserve answerability to honesty under burden.

That means the controller may not lawfully continue merely because the answer looks clear if the candidate has already fallen below the corridor’s honesty answerability requirements.

This matters because one of the most flattering controller lies is “composed honesty theater.”
A system sounds careful, but the legal honesty floor has already been weakened.

The packed master explicitly blocks that by keeping honesty-floor answerability non-erasable even under softer profile variation. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}

---

## 🟡 open_item_block_threshold

`open_item_block_threshold` exists because open items are not just documentation leftovers.
They can be controller-relevant if a candidate’s smooth surface starts hiding that the answer still depends on unresolved openness that has not been lawfully carried.

This threshold matters because it helps prevent a common fraud:

1. the answer sounds stable
2. the output feels complete
3. the unresolved open items become visually invisible
4. the controller quietly lets the passage through

The block exists to keep open-item truth from being polished away.

---

## 🚫 unsupported_claim_block_threshold

`unsupported_claim_block_threshold` exists because unsupported claims are one of the most dangerous late-stage failures precisely when the output looks most convincing.

This block matters because a candidate may still have:

1. high local fluency
2. strong payload density
3. composed structure
4. smooth public posture

and still remain unsupported in a way that makes lawful passage impossible.

The packed master explicitly treats unsupported-claim blocking as controller-side law rather than as optional afterthought.

---

## 🚫 fake_finality_block_threshold

`fake_finality_block_threshold` protects the corridor against one of the most persuasive controller frauds:

a candidate that feels finished enough to suppress further legality checks.

This matters because composed endings, stable headings, and smooth public posture can make the output look more complete than it actually is.

The block exists to stop controller passage from being purchased by completion vibes.

---

## 🧷 hidden_parent_exposure_block_threshold

`hidden_parent_exposure_block_threshold` protects against another subtle failure:

the candidate starts exposing or implying parent-grade authority or hidden parent structure in ways that are not lawfully bounded.

This matters because later-body polish can smuggle authority leakage under the guise of clarity or maturity.

The controller block exists so that a candidate may not pass merely because the leakage is elegant.

---

## 🛟 surface_rescue_after_legality_failure_block

This threshold family matters because some candidates try to rescue a legality failure with surface improvement.

They become:

1. cleaner
2. more publishable
3. more public-facing
4. more emotionally balanced
5. more readable

And a weak system begins to think the failure is now compensated.

The packed master explicitly rejects that story.
A surface rescue may be graceful and still remain unlawful.

This is one of the deepest anti-polish laws in the whole controller family.

## 🔁 Recursive revision remains subordinate

The controller region also preserves bounded recursion discipline and anti-runaway-control law.

That means recursive revision is allowed.
It may even be required.

But recursive revision may not become a tunnel around controller legality.

In other words:

1. more revision is not more permission
2. more careful iteration is not automatic passage
3. more text is not more legality
4. endless sensible continuation is still runaway if lawful stop has already been crossed

This matters because long-form systems often fail through respectable persistence rather than obvious collapse. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}

---

## 🚫 Controller legality is not ranking

Part 5D explicitly allows numeric attachment and explicitly refuses to let it become sovereign passage by score alone.

That means thresholds may support controller reading.
They may not replace controller legality.

This matters because once thresholds exist, a weak system begins telling itself:

1. score high enough = continue
2. score low enough = stop
3. the rest is basically tuning detail

The packed master rejects that collapse.

The thresholds are real.
But they remain subordinate to corridor legality, action-family distinction, anti-false-completion law, and honesty answerability. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}

## 🎚️ hard_control_profile

The packed master also preserves a launchpad-facing profile note for hard control.

`hard_control_profile` may lawfully vary across:

1. `minimum`
2. `baseline`
3. `standard`
4. `strong`

That note matters because it shows how the controller family may be tuned **without** being made casually switchable.

The profile may lawfully:

1. alter controller posture severity within lawful profile range
2. support replay comparison across stricter and lighter controller pressure
3. support threshold-family testing without deleting controller identity
4. support stage-bounded profile calibration for public-emission posture

But it may **not** lawfully:

1. switch hard control into unsupported absence
2. convert controller legality into optional decoration
3. authorize public emission by profile softness alone
4. collapse legality into score-only ranking
5. erase honesty-floor answerability
6. erase block-threshold answerability

This is one of the clearest anti-naive-control rules in the whole page. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

## 🚫 Profile softness is not permission

This has to be said directly.

A lighter controller profile is not a permission slip.

It does not mean:

1. weaker legality
2. softer honesty
3. relaxed unsupported-claim blocking
4. erased open-item discipline
5. automatic public emission

The packed master explicitly says profile variation governs controller posture only and remains subordinate to the constitutional legality of the corridor.

That sentence is doing a lot of work.
It means profile is real, but law remains prior. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

## 🔽 Downstream consequences

Part 5D also explicitly says later profile, SRD, engineering, reduction, and preservation sections remain downstream of controller legality.

That matters because the controller is not merely a late-stage style adjustment.
It is one of the last real legality-bearing decision corridors before later realization and release surfaces appear.

So later richness may still be blocked by controller truth.
Later engineering smoothness may still be blocked by controller truth.
Later reduction or release posture may still be blocked by controller truth.

This is exactly why controller law needs its own threshold page rather than being left implicit in other layers. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}
